Buffett News
Buffett to play the palace -- of Versailles
By Thom Smith, Palm Beach Post Staff Columnist
Wednesday, June 9, 2004

Don't be surprised if you see a couple of Gypsies in the Palace headlines out of France this week. Our favorite troubadour, Jimmy Buffett, is back in Paris, and he's not just "looking for answers to questions that bother him so."

Buffett will get in a baker's dozen of summer concerts stateside, wrapping with two at Boston's Fenway Park on Sept. 10 and 12, but the intriguing show is not on the official tour list. Improbable as it may seem, on Saturday night he'll play the palace of Versailles.

Pirate's persona notwithstanding, Buffett is a man of considerable refinement. He's a member of the American Friends of Versailles, an organization dedicated to restoring and preserving that grand palace. The Friends are throwing a ball so big that New York society sheets have proclaimed it "the most magnificent ball of the century." Some of the tickets are going for $1 million each, and the guest list includes first lady Laura Bush, Madam Bernadette Chirac, Lee Radziwill, Anne Bass, Princess Michael of Kent, Henry and Nancy Kissinger and astronaut Buzz Aldrin. Buffett will provide the music.

Actually Jimmy left for Europe last week because he had another, more personal mission, to visit the D-Day battleground. "He wanted to see Normandy," a friend said. "He'd never been there."

Shortly after Buffett returns, July 13 to be exact, his new album, License To Chill, will hit the shelves. It's full of country tunes and collaborations with the likes of George Strait, Alan Jackson, Kenny Chesney, Clint Black, Toby Keith, Martina McBride, Bill Withers and Nancy Griffith.

Fans got a taste of it two Saturdays ago at Texas Stadium near Dallas.

"There's a fine line between Parrotheads and rednecks," Strait observed as he took the stage in the uncharacteristic role of second banana. Just as wacky: The opener was Jackson.

"Imagine Strait or Jackson opening for anybody!" a member of Buffett's entourage said. "But it was quite a show."

He brought Jackson out for a run at It's Five O'Clock Somewhere, one of the year's top country songs that seems destined to become a crossover anthem. For an encore, he rolled out his new country single, Hank Williams' Hey, Good Lookin'. Country, rock, pop, oldies - - there was a time when radio stations couldn't figure Buffett out. Obviously to fans, it didn't matter.




Nautical Roots of Common Phrases, Part 1
The Vikings, Columbus, the Pilgrims... they all arrived here by ship. So it stands to reason that some of the phrases we use today were born on the high seas. While sources differ on the roots of many phrases, others have a clear path to the days of sailing across the ocean. Here's a look at a few family-friendly phrases that likely came from the mouths of sailors.

"Three Sheets to the Wind"
Many people are surprised to learn that this expression for drunkenness was born on the high seas. "Sheet" is the nautical term for the rope that controls the tension on a square sail. If the sheets are loose on a three-masted ship, then the sails will flap uselessly in the wind, and the ship will drift out of control until the situation is corrected. Thus, the modern phrase "three sheets to the wind" has come to signify a person who is intoxicated to the point of being out of control.

"By and Large"
A sailing vessel was considered seaworthy if it could sail both "by" (into the wind) and "large" (with the wind). This term has come to mean "approximately" in modern parlance.

"Slush Fund"
Most people think that this term originated in the smoke-filled boardrooms of corporate America. Surprisingly, however, it can be traced back to some clever ship cooks who saved the slushy mix of fat and grease that was left over after every meal. The slush would be stowed away in a secret hiding place until the ship returned to port. The cooks would then sell the fat to candle makers and other merchants, earning themselves a tidy sum in the process. Thus, the term "slush fund" refers to an illicit cash reserve.
